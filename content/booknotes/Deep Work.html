📒 Summary + Notes

Introduction

Deep Work: Professional activities performed in a state of distraction-free concentration that push your cognitive capabilities to their limit. These efforts create new value, improve your skill, and are hard to replicate.

If instead of writing a ton of short messages (like DMs) one spent their time consecutively they could write a novel.

All influential and successful people use deep work.

Shallow Work: Noncognitively demanding, logistical-style tasks, often performed while distracted. These efforts tend to not create much new value in the world and are easy to replicate.

The Deep Work Hypothesis: The ability to perform deep work is becoming increasingly rare at exactly the same time it is becoming increasingly valuable in our economy. As a consequence, the few who cultivate this skill, and then make it the core of their working life, will thrive.

Chapter One: Deep Work Is Valuable

Three type of people will become more valuable as the economy changes to an intelligent machine age.

  1. High-Skilled Workers: Work well with the machines
  1. Super stars: Are the best at what they do
  1. Owners: Have access to capital

To get into one of the first 2 groups of people we need two skills:

  1. The ability to quickly master hard things
  1. The ability to produce at an elite level, in terms of both quality and speed

To learn quickly and to produce at an elite level requires deep work.

High-Quality Work Produced = Time Spent x Intensity of Focus

Chapter Two: Deep Work Is Rare

Metric Black Hole, It’s impossible for a business to specifically measure the impacts of deep and shallow work which is why shallow work is so prevalent and deep work is so rare. There aren’t clear metrics indicating that deep work will bring more profit.

“Clarity about what matters provides clarity about what does not”

Busyness as Proxy for Productivity: In the absence of clear indicators of what it means to be productive and valuable in their jobs, many knowledge workers turn back toward an industrial indicator of productivity: doing lots of stuff in a visible manner.

Deep work is rare because its advantages cannot easily be measured, because shallow work is easier and makes us appear more productive and things associated with the internet (emails and instant messages) are associated with being good in today’s society.

Chapter Three: Deep Work Is Meaningful

Referencing programming (or to any physical or cognitive task): “Within the overall structure of a project there is always room for individuality and craftsmanship…One hundred years from now, our engineering may seem as archaic as the techniques used by medieval cathedral builders seem to today’s civil engineers, while our craftsmanship will still be honored.

Depth resonates at a deeper human level than the points argued previously as to why it’s important.

Depth has a strong connection with meaning, with producing, with achieving and succeeding

“A deep life is a good life”

Part 2: The Rules

Rule #1: Work Deeply

“You have a finite amount of willpower that becomes depleted as you use it.”

Therefore we have to be careful how we use our willpower and should form a schedule/habits that minimize willpower spent while still being able to do deep work. This will also preserve more mental energy for the deep work itself.

There are a couple approaches for this:

  1. Monastic Philosophy: Always in a state of deep work constantly removing distractions. Someone who works deep year round and every hour of the day. No emails, no social media.
  1. Bimodal Philosophy: Alternating between long stretches (often full days or weeks) and times where one isn’t doing deep work. This could be 4 days of deep work followed by 3 regular days or alternating every week or every month.
  1. Rhythmic Philosophy: Doing deep work for a couple hours every day then returning to normal work. This is easier to fit in a general office schedule where one has other expectations to meet.
  1. Journalistic Philosophy: To do deep work whenever it’s possible. This requires a high level of skill to switch into a deep work mode and a great confidence in one’s own ability to achieve with their deep work. It’s best suited for those with an unpredictable schedule.

To achieve deep work people will form habits to make the process easier. To have set times to work deep without distractions.

Some other people will make a radical change such as reserving a hotel just to work which will force them to work deeper. The psychology of committing to a deep task is what allows people to achieve great things during their deep work.

Disciplines:

  1. Focus on the Wildly Important: Instead of focusing on a lot of important things focus on a few very important ones.
  1. Focus on Lead Measures: Things you can measure that are within your direct control. Most notably: “hours spent in deep work state”
  1. Keep a Compelling Scoreboard: Keep an easy to see scoreboard of your lead measure. If working on a team have everyone contribute to it to stir competition.
  1. Create a Cadence of Accountability: If there’s a team of people with wildly important goals. They should have short regular meetings discussing where they expect to be on their scoreboard by the next meeting and how the previous goals they set turned out.

Why being lazy and relaxing sometime is beneficial to deep work (have a strict endpoint to your work day after which you no longer ‘work’ check emails, etc):

  1. Downtime Aids Insights: While your conscious mind is relaxing, the unconscious mind can tackle more complex problems and come up with solutions.
  1. Downtime Helps Recharge the Energy Needed to Work Deeply: Relaxing helps you focus deeper next time by recharging the finite resource that is your focus.
  1. The Work that Evening Downtime Replaces is Usually Not That Important: The work one does in the evening instead of taking time off is usually not deep work that brings value. Research shows that humans have a limit of deep work they can do each day.

When going for downtime after a day of work, it’s important to have a plan for how you’ll pick up so that work thoughts don’t flood your mind and distract you.

Rule #2: Embrace Boredom

When your brain is distracted too much for a long period of time (of social media or trying to multitask, etc) the brain loses the ability to enter a state of laser focus.

Being able to work deeply is something that has to be trained, it’s not innate in everyone.

IE: If every 5 minutes of waiting like at a restaurant waiting for a friend or for a bus leaves you on your phone then your brain is probably already in a “mental wreck” and you can’t focus anymore.

“Don’t take breaks from distraction. Instead take breaks from focus.”

Strategy: Schedule clear blocks where you use the internet and distractions and spend the rest of your time away from it.

  1. This strategy works even if your job requires lots of Internet use and/or prompt e-mail replies.
  1. Regardless of how you schedule your Internet blocks, you must keep the time outside these blocks absolutely free from Internet use. If you absolutely must use the Internet (ie: pressing work problem and must search up answer) during a no Internet block, then wait 5 minutes at least before searching it up as this will train your brain into knowing there’s resistance on going online. Avoid at all costs though, if you can work on something else that doesn’t require the Internet.
  1. Scheduling Internet use at home as well as at work can further improve your concentration training.

Sometimes we’ll want to distract ourselves, but to be bored can be rewarding in many ways in cultivating thoughts, and relaxing. (Veritasium video on this); the more you resist such urges the easier it is to resist.

Roosevelt, used to study at Harvard and do quite well while only studying for very little time, because every time he studied he was in utmost focus.

To setup: Have tasks that you must complete before a timer runs out (or commit publicly to the deadline) and make the time very hard to achieve requiring your full focus to succeed. Have one of those concentration sprints once a week to start to train yourself and slowly increase the frequency. The deadline should be so hard to reach that any amount of distraction whatsoever would make you fail.

Productive meditation: To take time where your mind isn’t doing anything to focus on a problem in your mind and meditate on it. Similar to mindfulness meditation your attention should return to the problem whenever you go off track. This will help build your distraction-resisting muscles.

Rule #3: Quit Social Media

The Any-Benefit Approach to Network Tool Selection: You’re justified in using a network tool if you can identify any possible benefit to its use, or anything you might possibly miss out on if you don’t use it.

Obviously this is wrong as it doesn’t consider the cons of a tool and only its pros.

The Craftsman Approach to Tool Selection: Identify the core factors that determine success and happiness in your professional and personal life. Adopt a tool only if its positive impacts on these factors substantially outweigh its negative impacts.

The goal here is to abandon the any-benefit mindset and apply the craftsman approach more often

List a few professional and personal goals. (They should be slightly general and not specific down to numbers) Then for each goal list 2-3 supporting activities that will make achieving this goal possible. For each activity figure out if the use of network tools, (social media, internet, etc) has a net positive or negative on the task.

The reason why we should focus on less goals and make them more specific is because of the law of the vital few.

The Law of the Vital Few: In many settings, 80 percent of a given effect is due to just 20 percent of the possible causes.

Quit all social media for 30 days (without announcing it to others) then ask yourself:

  1. Would the last thirty days have been notably better if I had been able to use this service?
  1. Did people care that I wasn’t using this service?

Then decide whether or not to keep that service.

Instead of Internet entertainment (reddit, youtube, etc) consider planning the day outside of working hours so that you have something else and specific to do that is both relaxing and specific.

Rule #4: Drain the Shallows

“For most businesses, if you eliminated significant amounts of this shallowness, their bottom line would likely remain unaffected.”

“Treat shallow work with suspicion because its damage is often vastly underestimated and its importance vastly overestimated.”

People underestimate the amount of time wasted/in the shallows and overestimate the time spent doing deep work.

Schedule Every Minute: Make a schedule down to the minute of blocks of tasks you want to do throughout your day. Use a page of a lined notebook and have the times written out on the left side. You might sometimes be forced to change the schedule which is fine. You can cross out the current task blocks and make a new schedule on the right of it if you can’t finish a task in time or something pops up that requires attention.

This is not for sticking to a schedule constantly, but rather to treat your time with respect.

To figure out if a task is deep or shallow work ask yourself: “How long would it take (in months) to train a smart recent college graduate with no specialized training in my field to complete this task?”

The more months the deeper the task; you should prioritize tasks that are deep.

Then decide on a fixed ratio of shallow vs deep work (usually around 30 to 50%).

Fixed Schedule Mindset: The mindset that you’re only going to work for so many hours a day or a week. And that any time taken away from it doing shallow work probably isn’t the best. This leads one to reject shallow work such as “jump on a call” and stuff. This usually means after a certain time (say 5:30) you just don’t work anymore and take the time to relax. (no answering emails, etc)

Become Hard to Reach:

  1. Make People Who Send You E-mail Do More Work: Add a sender filter, make those who want to email you filtered out by lowering their expectation of getting a response and letting them know that you only care about important emails. Set their expectations so that you probably won’t respond: “I’ll only respond to those proposals that are a good match for my schedule and interests.” It’s psychologically freeing to feel like you don’t have to answer all your emails.
  1. Do More Work When You Send or Reply to E-mails: For many emails people get they’ll try to quickly dash out a response to get it out of their inbox as soon as possible. However, this might be counter-productive as the lack of details and advancement on their reply might actually lead to more back and forth to figure things out. Try asking yourself: “What is the project represented by this message, and what is the most efficient (in terms of messages generated) process for bringing this project to a successful conclusion?”
  1. Don’t Respond: Not worth the time if the email is ambiguous, not a question or interesting proposal, or nothing really good would happen if you did respond and nothing really bad would happen if you didn’t.

Conclusion

“The ability to concentrate is a skill that gets valuable things done.”

Deep work allowed Cal Newport to go from 4 published academic papers to 9 + publishing a book in 1 year.
