1. Optimal Stopping

Scenario:

You interview applicants for a job in random order, one at a time. You can decide to offer the job to an applicant at any point an they are guaranteed to accept, terminating the search. But if you pass over an applicant, deciding not to hire them, they are gone forever.

Answer: We should look for 37% of the time then take the next best applicant after that line. This gives us a 37% chance of picking the best applicant too.

This scenario has variations where you might be rejected 50% of the time in which case you should offer at around 25% of the way through.

Similarly, if there‚Äôs a 50% chance an old applicant would still accept then the looking period should get extended to 61% of the time (giving a 61% chance of picking the best person). If you just interviewed a person they‚Äôre still guaranteed to accept your offer.

If you have ‚Äúfull information‚Äù ie: which percentile your applicant is in, then you should use the Threshold Rule, where we immediately accept an applicant if she is above a certain percentile.

This only depends on how much waiting for one offer costs and the range where you can possibly get offers. For 2000%/offer and a range of 400k to 500k then wait until there‚Äôs an offer above 480k. 2. Explore/Exploit

‚ÄúEvery day we are constantly forced to make decisions between options that differ in a very specific dimension: do we try new things or stick with our favorite ones? ‚Ä¶ Life is a balance between novelty and tradition, the latest and the greatest, between taking risks and savoring what we know and love.‚Äù

The explore, exploit problem is one where we have limited time and must spend some time gathering information (exploring) and some time using that information (exploiting). We wish to strike the balance that helps us out the most.

An example would be: being in a casino with just 2 slot machines each with different odds of payouts. You want to play them a bit to know which one is better then spend the rest of your time playing the better slot machine.

When you have a lot of time left, it‚Äôs better to explore a bit more, but when time is running out it‚Äôs best to start cashing in with the best you‚Äôve found so far.

This problem is unsolved, but a general strategy is that if you win then you stay. (Win-stay)

‚ÄúTo try and fail is at least to learn; to fail to try is to suffer the inestimable loss of what might have been‚Äù

A decent human solution to this problem is to consider what would minimize regret. Think or yourself as the 80 year old looking back on your life.

Actually minimizing regret is solved to be regret increasing at a logarithmic rate.

Basically this relates to confidence intervals in statistics, as a machine that hasn‚Äôt been played that many times might have the same expected value as a machine that was played more, but have larger confidence intervals.

The Upper Confidence Bound algorithm tells us to pick the slot machine with the highest top of the confidence interval. This is an optimistic algorithm that considers what a slot machine might realistically give out as best choice.

‚ÄúIn the long run, optimism is the best prevention for regret.‚Äù

A/B testing: Where a company can put out different versions of a website to different random set of users and see if their interactions are statistically different. Metrics such as engagement, money spent, etc are tracked and subtle tweaks are made to optimize.

This also shows itself in how old and young people deal with life. Young people like exploring a lot as they have so much time left to live, but old people would prefer exploiting whatever they have accumulated throughout their life.

5. Scheduling

‚ÄúHow we spend our days is, of course, how we spend our lives.‚Äù

Computer science strives on making explicit clear cut goals.

Strategy 1: Earliest Due Date, always do the work that is due earliest. (minimizes maximum lateness)

Strategy 2: Shortest Processing Time, always do the quickest task. This will minimize the sum of the completion times

Strategy 3: Divide the weight of the task by the time it takes then apply shortest processing time. This will reduce the weights of the to-do list as fast as possible.

The problems then get added complexity by considering what would happen if some tasks must be completed before other tasks. (Most problems with added complexity becomes unsolvable)

Sometimes we can leave a task half finished and return to it later, then even if some tasks can only be started after a certain time the strategies above still work.

However, this task switching for humans and computers alike isn‚Äôt free. It takes us precious time to get back into our work flow often costing us minutes every time we switch.

Sometimes the task of figuring out a schedule is itself a task to be schedule and takes time.

Responsiveness: How often you have to check if a switch is required. If you have to pay credit card bills once a month, then pay all of them on one day. If you need to respond to emails in less than 24hours then check once a day.

The idea of interrupting coalescence is to block all the similar interruptions together. (office hours, running errands, etc)

6. Bayes‚Äôs Rule
