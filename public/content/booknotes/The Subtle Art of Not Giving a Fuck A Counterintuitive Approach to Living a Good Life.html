📒 Summary + Notes

Chapter 1: Don’t Try

The world wants us to give more fucks about everything, but “the key to a good life is not giving a fuck about more; it’s giving a fuck about less, giving a fuck about only what is true and immediate and important.”

In modern society, the constant flood of happy, successful people from the internet makes us feel bad about feeling bad.

“The desire for more positive experience is itself a negative experience. And, paradoxically, the acceptance of one’s negative experience is itself a positive experience.”

“The avoidance of suffering is a form of suffering. The avoidance of struggle is a struggle. The denial of failure is a failure. Hiding what is shameful is itself a form of shame.”

“Like the road not taken, it’s the fucks not given that made all the difference.”

“To not give a fuck is to stare down life’s most terrifying and difficult challenges and still take action. […] Most of us struggle throughout our lives by giving too many fucks in situations where fucks do not deserve to be given.”

If you go around giving a fuck about everything, then you’re gonna get fucked.

Subtlety #1: Not giving a fuck does not mean being indifferent; it means being comfortable with being different: it’s not not caring about anything, it’s not caring about the adversity in the face of your goals. Not caring about pissing some people off to do what feels right or important or noble.

Subtlety #2: To not give a fuck about adversity, you must first give a fuck about something more important than adversity.

Subtlety: #3 Whether you realize it or not, you are always choosing what to give a fuck about.

“The only way to overcome pain is to first learn how to bear it”

Chapter 2: Happiness Is a Problem

“The solution to one problem is merely the creation of the next one”

“Happiness comes from solving problems” It’s not having problems, or avoiding them, it’s actively solving them that makes us happy.

“Negative emotions are a call to action. When you feel them, it’s because you’re supposed to do something”

Don’t underestimate or overestimate your emotions.

“Whatever makes us feel good will also inevitably make us feel bad. What we gain is also what we lose. What creates our positive experiences will define our negative experiences.”

Everyone basically wants the same things in life. (Fame, money, house, car, family, partner, etc) The more important question though is “what pain do you want in your life?”

“Happiness requires struggle. It grows from problems.”

Our struggles determine our successes, the joy is not at the top, but in the climb itself.

Chapter 3: You Are Not Special

“A true measurement of self-worth is not how a person feels about her positive experiences, but rather how she feels about her negative experiences.”

“because he can’t face his problems, no matter how good he feels about himself, he is weak.”

In cases of traumatic shit: When we feel like we can’t solve our problems in life we become miserable and helpless. But unconsciously we start believing that we’re uniquely special or defective in some way that no one else is. This causes us to become entitled through

  1. I’m awesome and the rest of you all such, so I deserve special treatment
  1. I suck and the rest of you are all awesome, so I deserve special treatment

Often switching between those two options when we’re entitled/selfish, but those problems are not special. Millions suffer from them and having them doesn’t make us privileged. Not everyone is going to be extraordinary, most of us are average, mediocre, etc. “The knowledge and acceptance of your own mundane existence will actually free you to accomplish what you truly wish to accomplish, without judgment or lofty expectations.” We should appreciate life’s basic experiences, the things that sound boring, that everyone can have, that is ordinary. “But may they’re ordinary for a reason: because they are what actually matters.”

Chapter 4: The Value of Suffering

We all suffer, so we should choose to suffer for a meaningful cause and to know why we suffer.

Three layers of self-awareness:

  1. Identify how you’re feeling.
  1. Figure out why you’re feeling that way. (usually because of a success/failure)
  1. Find our personal values. (why do you think that thing was a failure/success)

values decide how we measure our problems

“One day, in retrospect, the years of struggle will strike you as the most beautiful”

Good values are: Reality base, socially constructive, immediate and controllable.

When we have poor values we give fucks about things that don’t matter. When we have better values we divert our fucks to something better.

Chapter 5: You Are Always Choosing

“When we feel that we’re choosing our problems, we feel empowered. When we feel that your problems are being forced upon us against our will, we feel victimized and miserable.”

“To not give a fuck about anything is still to give a fuck about something.”

“With great responsibility comes great power.”

We are responsible for experiences that aren’t our fault, and isn’t in our control all the time and have to deal with it and choose what we do about them.

“To simply blame others is only to hurt yourself.”

You’re responsible for how you react to events. To the emotions you feel after things happen.

Chapter 6: You’re Wrong About Everything (But So Am I)

“We don’t actually know what a positive or negative experience is. Some of the most difficult and stressful moments of our lives also end up being the most formative and motivating. Some of the best and most gratifying experiences of our lives are also the most distracting and demotivating. Don’t trust your conception of positive/negative experiences. All that we know for certain is what hurts in the moment and what doesn’t. And that’s not worth much.”

“The fact that [you] do everything “right” doesn’t make [you] right.”

“Openness to being wrong must exist for any real change or growth to take place”

“The more something threatens your identity, the more you will avoid it.”

Questions to be less certain of yourself:

  1. What if I’m wrong? “We’re all the world’s worst observers of ourselves.”
  1. What would it mean if I were wrong?
  1. Would being wrong create a better or a worse problem than my current problem for both myself and others?

“If it’s down to me being screwed up or everybody else being screwed up, it is far, far, far more likely that I’m the one who’s screwed up. […] If it feels like it’s you versus the world, chances are it’s really just you versus yourself.”

Chapter 7: Failure Is The Way Forward

“If we’re unwilling to fail, then we’re unwilling to succeed.”

“Life is about not knowing and then doing something anyways”

“Action isn’t just the effect of motivation; it’s also the cause of it.”

“With simply doing something as your only metric for success—well, then even failure pushes you forward”

Chapter 8: The Importance of Saying No

But we need to reject something. Otherwise, we stand for nothing. If nothing is better or more desirable than anything else, then we are empty and our life is meaningless. We are without values and therefore live our life without any purpose. The avoidance of rejection (both giving and receiving it) is often sold to us as a way to make ourselves feel better. But avoiding rejection gives us short-term pleasure by making us rudderless and directionless in the long term.

When you confine yourself to one thing, (one person, career, craft, etc) you must then reject everything else in order to focus on it.

“We must all give a fuck about something to value something. And to value something we must reject what is not that something.”

Chapter 9: … And Then You Die

We all aim to outlive our physical self when we are faced with death. This means we choose to maintain some form of a legacy.

Death is kinda what makes life worth living
