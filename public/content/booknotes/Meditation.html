🚀 The Book in 3 Sentences

  1. Do good, but not to impress others, since you really aren’t that special of a person.
  1. Life isn’t that long, but even then we can spend our time doing good for society.
  1. I should judge others less, even if they judge me or speak ill of me.

🎨 Impressions

Language feels a bit old. Very repetitive, some very boring and long passages. The important parts are interesting.

How I Discovered It

Bought it from a gift store when I visited Italy.

Who Should Read It?

It’s a classic in stoicism

☘️ How the Book Changed Me

  • It’s making me slightly more religious/spiritual.
  • A lot of things don’t matter as much as we think they do.
  • I should judge others less, even if they judge me or speak ill of me.
  • To keep your ego in check and still do good.

📒 Summary + Notes

Book I

Modesty

“abstinence, not only from evil deeds, but even from evil thoughts”

“endurance of labor, and to want little, and to work with my own hands, and not to meddle with other people’s affairs, and not to be ready to listen to slander”

“not to breed quails for fighting, nor to give myself up passionately to such things; to endure freedom of speech”

“nor to showing myself off as a man who practices much discipline, or does benevolent acts in order to make a display”

write simple letters that get to the point

respect those who offended you

be disposed to be reconciled as long as others are ready to be reconciled

don’t give assent immediately to those who talk too much

“freedom of will and undeviating steadiness of purpose”

To not change who you are when you’re in pain

“how to receive from friends what are esteemed favors, without being either humbled by them or letting them pass unnoticed.”

“look carefully after the interest of friends, and to tolerate ignorant persons”

“refrain from fault-finding”

to not “chide” other’s accent

observe effect of envy in tyrants

“nor continually to excuse the neglect of duties required by our relations to those with whom we live, by alleging urgent occupations”

speak well of teachers

love my children truly

try to restore friends to their usual disposition when they’re at faults

love my kin

to love truth

to love justice

same law for all

“disposition to do good, and to give to others readily, and to cherish good hopes, and to believe that I am loved by my friends”

“No concealment of his opinions with respect to those whom he condemned, and that his friends had no need to conjecture what he wished or did not wish, but it was quite plain”

do what was set before me without complaining

nor did he ever laugh to disguise his vexation

“he was accustomed to do acts of beneficence, and was ready to forgive, and was free from all falsehood;”

unchangeable resolution

“he considered himself no more than any other citizen”

respected others ideas, but not easily led by them

took good care of his physical health, not for appearance, but to not rely on doctors

look at what ought to be done, not at the reputational risks and benefits

indebted to the gods (for every good person in his life)

Book 2

“still your soul reveres not itself, but seeks your well-being in the souls of others”

“how quickly all things disappear”

“the longest and shortest [lives] are thus brought to the same”

a man cannot lose the past or the future as how can one lose what one doesn’t have

Book 3

“we must make haste then, not only because we are daily nearer to death, but also because the conception of things and the understanding of them cease first”

“do not waste the remainder of your life in thoughts about others, when you do not refer your thoughts to some object of common utility. For you lose the opportunity of doing something else when you have such thoughts as these.”

“he does not value at all the praise that comes from such men, since they are not even satisfied with themselves”

“a man then must stand erect, not be kept erect by others”

“Never value anything as profitable that compels you to break your promise, to lose your self-respect, to hate any man, to suspect, to curse, to act the hypocrite, to desire anything that needs walls and curtains.”

“On every occasion a man should be able to say this comes from god”

“if you apply yourself to the task before you, following right reason seriously, vigorously, calmly, without allowing anything else to distract you, but keeping your divine part pure, as if you might be bound to give it back immediately…you will live happily.”

Book 4

“Let no act be done without a purpose”

“there is no retreat that is quieter or freer from trouble than a man’s own soul, especially when he has within him such thoughts that by looking into them he is immediately in perfect tranquillity”

Find a calmness within yourself to brace yourself against anything you find in life.

“It is natural that certain things should be done by a certain kind of person”

“everything that happens, happens justly”

many grains — one falls before, one falls after, but it makes no difference

“Do not act as if you were going to live ten thousand years. Death hangs over you. While you live, while it is in your power, be good.”

Don’t look around at the morals of others, but follow your own straightly.

What good does it give you that you remain famous well after your death? It has no effect on your dead self, and arguably no effect on your living being.

Be a man who is satisfied with his portion of the whole, and satisfied with his benevolence.

The world constantly changes: “It is no evil for things to undergo change, and no good for things to subsist in consequence of change.”

“Not that this is misfortune, but that to bear it nobly is good fortune.” (Unfortunate things happen to everyone, that is not misfortune, but not everyone can deal with it well, so dealing with it well is good fortune.)

“Look to the immensity of time behind you and to the time that is ahead of you, another boundless space. In this infinity, then, what is the difference between him who lives three days and him who lives three generations?”

Book 5

When you rise in the morning, you do so to do the work of a human being, no matter how hard that is. You weren’t made to stay under your blankets all day long. If you do not follow your natural order to rise and do the work, then you do not love yourself. You do not love your own nature.

“Do not be disgusted, discouraged, or dissatisfied if you do not succeed in doing everything according to right principles; but when you have failed, return again, and be content if the greater part of what you do is consistent with man’s nature, and love this to which you return;”

“where a man can live, there he can also live well”

“you must not be angry with him who does harm the state. Show him where his error is”

We’re a very small part of the whole universal substance and time.

Behave in such a way that everyone around you says this of you, “Never has he wronged a man in deed or word.”

Why are you troubled by something that you wasn’t caused by your own badness, and doesn’t harm the common weal?

Book 6

Whether you’re cold or warm or drowsy or satisfied with sleep or ill-spoken of or praised, you should still do your duty.

The universe is not evil in itself

All existing things will change and be gone

“If it is difficult to accomplish something by yourself, do not think that it is impossible for man: but if anything is possible for man and conformable to his nature, think that this can be attained by you, too.”

If you get hurt by someone and they weren’t intentionally trying to hurt you. Don’t show any signs of vexation or be offended, nor should you suspect them as a treacherous fellow. However be on guard against him.

“If any man is able to convince me and show me that I do not think or act right, I will gladly change; for I seek the truth by which no man was ever injured. But he is injured who abides in his error and ignorance.”

You must finish your duty and not be disturbed or show anger towards those who are angry with you.

“how he bore with those who blamed him unjustly without blaming them in return”

“You are not dissatisfied, I suppose, because, you weigh only so many pounds and not three hundred. Do not be dissatisfied then that you must live only so many years and not more; for as you are satisfied with the amount of substance that has been assigned to you, so be content with the time.”

What is good for you? Those who love fame think it is that of other’s admiring him. Those who love pleasure think it’s their own sensations. But those who understand consider their acts good.

“He who loves fame considers another man’s activity to be his own good; and he who loves pleasures, his own sensations; but he who has understanding considers his own acts to be his own good.”

Book 7

“Do not be ashamed to be helped; for it is your business to do your duty.” No shame in being helped if you’re doing what you must do.

Don’t be discouraged by the future.

“Be upright, or be made upright.”

Change is always necessary, burning wood for a hot bath, or digesting food are all changes. So do not be afraid of changes in yourself for it is the play of the universal nature.

Be satisfied by the peace that your own mind brings

Pardon easily and pity those who have done wrong, but do not blame them as it’s often not their fault.

Consider the universal scale, of stars moving around, galaxies forming and dying and use it to purge away the filth of our earthly life

“We must endure, and toil without complaining.”

“Consider yourself to be dead, and to have completed your life up to the present time; and live, according to nature, the remainder that is allowed you.”

Once you’ve done a good act to someone, do not expect anything in return as the reputation (of knowing yourself) that you did good should be enough to you.

Book 8

“Pleasure then is neither good nor useful”

“Attend to the matter before you, whether it is an opinion or an act or a word.”

To let your soul think that pain is an evil is an evil in itself as the soul should maintain its own “serenity and tranquility”

It’s in your power to not let bad things affect you internally

Everyone dies.

“Receive wealth or prosperity without arrogance; and be ready to let it go.”

“It is not fit that I should give myself pain, for I have never intentionally given pain even to another.”

“And what is it in any way to you if these men of tomorrow utter this or that sound, or have this or that opinion about you.” Don’t search posthumous fame?

“Therefore the mind that is free from passions is a citadel, for man has nothing more secure to which he can fly for refuge and repel every attack.”

“He who fears death fears either the loss of sensation or a different kind of sensation. But if you shall have no sensation, neither will you feel any harm; and if you will acquire another kind of sensation, you will be a different kind of living being and you will not cease to live.”

Book 9

“He who does wrong does wrong against himself. He who acts unjustly acts unjustly to himself, because he makes himself bad.”

“in wars, there are treaties and armistices”

All trouble is from within.

When others blame you or hate you, analyze what kind of person they are and discover that there’s no reason to be concerned about their opinions of you. However, still be well disposed toward them.

If the gods don’t exist or have no power, there’s no point praying to them. If they do exist, then why do we assume they have the power to grant us things we want. Why not instead pray for the gods to give us the power to achieve our goals or not be distracted. For example “One may pray thus: How might I sleep with that woman? Do you pray: How shall I not desire to sleep with her? Another prays thus: How shall I be released from this? Another prays: How shall I not desire to be released?”

If you’re offended by a man’s shameless behaviour, ask yourself if shameless should not be in the world. Obviously, there’s always going to be shameless men. Then, this man is just acting of their nature and you have the ability to oppose every wrongful act. You may teach the man who has gone astray.

When you’re kind as a man, do not act like you deserve something for acting kind. It’s of the nature of a man to be kind and grateful, the same that eyes are for seeing and feet are for walking, yet they do not get paid.

Book 10

“Remember, however, that you are formed by nature to bear everything whose tolerability depends on your opinion to make it so, by thinking that it is in your interest or duty to do so.”

Remember that you are a part of nature, that follows strict causality laws.

Assume the name of being “good”, “modest”, “truthful”, “rational”, “equanimity” and “magnanimous” without wanting to hear others call you that.

Equanimity: voluntary acceptance of the things that were assigned to you by the common nature

Magnanimity: elevation from pleasure or pain of the flesh and above fame, and death.

“Inquire of yourself as soon as you wake from sleep, whether it will make any difference to you if another does what is just and right. It will make no difference.”

“No longer talk at all about the kind of man that a good man ought to be, but be such.”

“When you are offended at any man’s fault, immediately turn to yourself and reflect in what manner you yourself have erred”

“be free from grief and fear”

The healthy ought to be prepared for everything that happens and can recognize truth for what it is. The unhealthy wish that truth and reality be different. (An unhealthy eye would wish to have seen differently)

Book 11

The soul must be ready to leave the body anytime, in a way without making it seem tragic

“Have I done something for the general interest? Well then I have had my reward.”

Doing the good is the reward

“If the things do not come to you, the pursuit and avoidance of which disturb you, still in a manner you are seeking them out. Let then your judgment about them be at rest, and they will remain quiet, and you will not be seen either pursuing or avoiding”

Must show it by acts, not by saying it

“consider that you also do many things wrong”

Book 12

“I have often wondered how it is that every man loves himself more than all the rest of men, but yet sets less value on his own opinion of himself than on the opinion of others.”

“If it is not right, do not do it: if it is not true, do not say it. For let your impulse be in your own power.”
