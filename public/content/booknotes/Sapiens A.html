2. The Tree of Knowledge

From around 70,000 years ago to 30,000 years ago, humans developed boats, oil lambs, bows, arrows and needles as well as art, religion, commerce and social stratification.

This was a period of Cognitive Revolution. Likely caused by humans caring about having information of other humans, in other words gossip.

We can use our language to talk about things that do not exist. → collaborate

“Large numbers of strangers can cooperate successfully by believing in common myths.” those myths include, religion, nationality, money, laws, etc

This happens in modern humans too. We can create imaginary things by telling stories (that millions will believe in). We created imaginary entities that today are very powerful like the United States, Christianity, and Google.

  • Myths can be changed (faster than genes can at least) which opens a fast lane of cultural evolution allowing Sapiens “to revise its behaviour rapidly in accordance with changing needs”
  • Eg: Homo Erectus didn’t change its tools in 2 million years, in contrast Homo Sapiens developed a super wide variety of tools in the 30,000 years from the Cognitive Revolution.
  • Composing fiction allows us to work together better. eg: It allowed us to trade by believing in a common value, allowing us to trust strangers

“The immense diversity of imagined realities that Sapiens invented, and the resulting diversity of behaviour patterns are the main components of what we call ‘cultures’. Once cultures appeared, they never ceased to change and develop, and these unstoppable alterations are what we call ‘history’.”

Once the Cognitive Revolution took place, to understand humans required understanding their history as well as biology.

Part Two: The Agricultural Revolution

5. History’s Biggest Fraud

Around 10,000 years ago humans went from hunters and gatherers to sowing seeds, watering plants and plucking weeds. They became farmers 🧑‍🌾

Started around 9500-8500BC in south-eastern Turkey, western Iran and the Levant

  • Started with wheat, goats (9000BC)
  • then peas and lentils (8000BC),
  • then olive trees (5000BC),
  • horses (4000BC), and
  • grapevines (3500BC)

Almost no other species have been domesticated since 3500 BC, most of our modern food stems from this period of domestication.

People in other parts of the world discovered domestication independently at around the same time.

“Of the thousands of species that our ancestors hunted and gathered, only a few were suitable candidates for farming and herding”

Which is why we don’t see the domestication of mammoths in Alaska. So only a few places developed agriculture at first.

“Agricultural Revolution left farmers with lives generally more difficult and less satisfying than those of foragers. Hunter-gatherers spent their time in more stimulating and varied ways, and were less in danger of starvation and disease.”

From wheat’s perspective, the plant domesticated humans making itself one of the most successful plants ever.

The agricultural revolution made the average human’s life worse:

  • Less food, less diverse food
  • More likely to engage in battle against territory (death by violence)
  • More risk of starving due to natural phenomena
  • More tedious work, less diverse, and work that our bodies weren’t evolved for

But it benefited Homo sapiens the species. “Cultivating wheat provided much more food per unit of territory, and thereby enabled Homo sapiens to multiply exponentially.”

The species as a whole benefited, but which individual would voluntarily do that?

  • “Humans, like many mammals, have hormonal and genetic mechanisms that help control procreation. In good times females reach puberty earlier, and their chances of getting pregnant are a bit higher. In bad times puberty is late and fertility decreases.”
  • The global warming after the last ice age, caused more rainfall benefiting wheat
  • As humans gathered the wheat and brought it back to campsites to process it, a few seeds inevitably got spread
  • The burning of forests by humans also helped wheat grow.
  • This led to temporary camps by humans, and year by year humans probably stayed to gather wheat for longer
  • They developed more techniques for gathering the wheat until they started planting it too.
  • Not having to move around as much, humans started to procreate more.
  • Cause higher child mortality as diseases were more common and each child was looked after less by mothers. Feeding children with more porridge and less breast milk weakened their immune system
  • Farmers tried to make small improvements generation by generation not realizing that those improvements made life worse.
  • People thought - If we work harder, we’ll have an easier life tomorrow, but in reality the food was spread between more mouths, requiring them to work even harder.
  • Permanent settlements were hotbeds of disease, got weakened by droughts and were at risk of being attacked for their land and resources
  • Farmers couldn’t go back to hunting and gathering because: the land didn’t have enough resources to support the population increase, and hunting/gathering was so long ago that no one really remembered
  • (Even modern humans make the same mistakes in their personal careers)
  • “Humanity’s search for an easier life released immense forces of change that transformed the world in ways nobody envisioned or wanted.”

It’s also possible that due cultural reasons, foragers built temples and then had to settle down around the temples long term causing them to switch to agriculture.

Similar domestication happened for various animals

Even though from an evolutionary perspective a species might be successful, this often comes at the cost of the well-being of the individual organisms

7. Memory Overload

“Because the Sapiens social order is imagined, humans cannot preserve the critical information for running it simply by making copies of their DNA and passing these on to their progeny. A conscious effort has to be made to sustain laws, customs, procedures and manners, otherwise the social order would quickly collapse.”

Large societies generate a huge amount of economic data to gather taxes and track spending which developed into the early development of writing and math.

Writing was necessary for humans to pass down their myths that held society together as no one could memorize it all and the knowledge stored in a human brain disappeared after the individual’s death.

Full scripts like the Sumerian system (2500 BC), Egyptian hieroglyphics, China (1200BC), Central America (1000-500BC) started getting developed by adding to partial scripts

Full scripts spread out from these original centres taking on new forms and people began to write poetry history books, romances, dramas, etc

With only a partial script stories like the Bible, and Iliad could still be told by speaking as they aren’t too difficult to remember. The full script isn’t as crucial to a functioning society as a partial script is. (Today we can think of the binary system that computers run on as partial script)

Although we were able to keep records, those records were difficult to search in case of a dispute.

The cultures mentioned above kept their script by inventing techniques of archiving, cataloguing and retrieving written records, making them way more popular.

Although numbers are part of our script for a long time now, humans still don’t inherently have the genes that make them well suited towards working with numbers.

10. The Scent of Money

Hunter-gatherers did not use money. Instead each band was economically viable and shared between each other. If a certain item couldn’t be gathered they would trade with neighbouring bands that had access.

The issue with barter is that as an economy scales (say a large city), then the pairs of prices grows quadratically. Everyone must keep track of how the value of each item compares to each other item. Additionally, it requires that both people want what the other person has to offer. However, with money, the # of transaction pairs to keep track of is linear and everyone can sell and buy for money.

“Money is anything that people are willing to use in order to represent systematically the value of other things for the purpose of exchanging goods and services. Money enables people to compare quickly and easily the value of different commodities (such as apples, shoes and divorces), to easily excahnge one thing for another, and to store wealth conveniently.”

“more than 90% of all money — more than $50 trillion appearing in our accounts — exists only on computer servers”

“it’s even better than shiny coins and crips banknotes — lighter, less bulky, and easier to keep track of”

“Everyone always wants money because everyone else also always wants money, which means you can exchange money for whatever you want or need.”

“money is thus a universal medium of exchange that enables people to convert almost everything into almost anything else.”

“Ideal types of money enables people not merely to turn one thing into another, but to store wealth as well. Many valuables cannot be stored — such as time or beauty.” Some things are hard to store or transport, such as foods, real estate, etc. Money solves those problems.

“Because money can convert, store and transport wealth easily and cheaply, it made a vital contribution to the appearance of complex commercial networks and dynamic markets.”

“Money isn’t a material reality — it is a psychological construct.”

“whereas religion asks us to believe in something, money asks us to believe that other people believe in something”

Part Four: The Scientific Revolution

14. The Discovery of Ignorance

“Science needs more than just research to make progress. It depends on the mutual reinforcement of science, politics and economics. Political and economic institutions provide the resources without which scientific research is almost impossible.”

Modern science is different from previous endeavours from humans to obtain knowledge in three ways:

  1. The willingness to admit ignorance (no knowledge is sacred and all can be challenged)
  1. The centrality of observation and mathematics (use mathematical tools to connect observations into theories)
  1. The acquisition of new powers (develop new technology)

Before modern science people asserted that there was nothing more to learn and God gave humans all the required knowledge in the Bible, (similarly for other religions)

However, the fact that science admits its ignorance means that if it is ever changed it would change the myths that hold society together. Sociopolitical order has been conserved with 2 unscientific methods

  • Take a scientific theory and declare that it is a final and absolute truth.
  • Leave science out and live in accordance with a non-scientific absolute truth.

Science wasn’t always tied with the development of technology
